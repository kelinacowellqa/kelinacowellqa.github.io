🎯 My QA Role So Far

🛠️ Workflow & Communication

Testing began after the developer asked for feedback on onboarding, player guidance, object interaction, player feedback, and early engagement. The first QA pass was carried out from a completely new player perspective to assess whether the opening session clearly explained what Grove Bound wanted the player to do and why.

Later testing continued into post-tutorial progression and then into regression and feature validation after an updated build was released. This allowed me to check whether earlier player-facing concerns had improved and whether newly added systems were understandable during normal play.

🧠 How I Decided What to Test

The initial test strategy was based on the developer's stated concerns: onboarding, object interactions, lack of player feedback, information overload, look and feel, and whether the first few minutes encouraged players to continue.

I prioritised areas that could affect early retention: unclear goals, weak progression direction, hidden quest access, systems that were discoverable but not clearly useful, missing feedback, and new features that worked mechanically but did not yet communicate their purpose well enough to players.

Player-Focused Exploratory QA

I tested Grove Bound as a new player, focusing on what the game communicated naturally without external explanation. This included tutorial flow, first-session goals, NPC interaction, resource use, early systems, and whether the player had enough reason to keep going.

Regression & Feature Validation

After updates were made, I retested earlier friction areas and validated new buildable features. The focus was not just whether systems functioned, but whether their purpose, value, feedback, and progression role were clear during normal gameplay.

📈 Testing Lifecycle So Far

The QA work so far has moved through three main stages. Each pass had a different purpose rather than repeating the same coverage blindly.

1. First-time player onboarding pass Onboarding, tutorial flow, player guidance, feedback, fatigue, lantern communication, interaction clarity, and first-session engagement. Assess whether a new player could understand Grove Bound's core mechanics and opening flow without developer guidance.
2. Post-tutorial progression pass Quest-led exploration, crafting discovery, cooking discovery, new area progression, system purpose, and continued motivation after onboarding. Check whether players had a clear reason to keep playing once the tutorial had ended and more systems began opening up.
3. Regression and feature validation pass Tutorial improvements, progression guidance, quest guidance, crafting clarity, cooking clarity, buildable campfires, and storage crate validation. Validate whether previous concerns had improved and assess whether newly added buildable systems were understandable from a player perspective.
4. Ongoing update support Further regression testing, new feature validation, bug fix checks, storage system validation, buildable system review, and Steam demo readiness. Continue supporting the project as Grove Bound moves through active development toward a Steam demo release.

📈 Early Impact So Far

After the developer implemented several onboarding, tutorial, crafting, and early progression improvements, he shared browser play data showing a visible increase in activity for the updated itch.io build, including a peak of 762 browser plays on 17/06/2026.

I would not present this as a direct one-to-one result of QA alone, because updates, timing, visibility, player interest, and other factors can all affect play numbers. However, it does show that the areas tested and improved during these early QA passes were connected to a stronger public update.

Browser play data shared by the developer after early onboarding, tutorial, crafting, and progression improvements were implemented.

This is included as supporting context, not as a claim that QA alone caused the increase. The useful point is that early player-facing improvements were made, then followed by stronger public activity on the updated build.

🤝 Working With the Developer

Grove Bound is a solo-dev indie project, so the QA support needs to be flexible, practical, and directly useful. Some passes are broad and exploratory. Others are focused on regression testing, specific features, or checking whether previous feedback has been successfully addressed.

I separate issues by player-facing impact rather than treating every observation as the same kind of bug. A progression blocker, unclear quest direction, missing feedback, confusing system purpose, and a minor dialogue polish issue all need different handling.

This helps the developer understand what matters most, what should be fixed before wider player exposure, what can be improved later, and what needs further discussion or shared classification.

The focus of this work is ongoing QA support: identifying player-facing risks, validating fixes, checking regressions, testing new systems, and helping Grove Bound become clearer and more demo-ready across updates.
